# Independent skeptical review

## Bottom line

I do **not** think the evidence yet justifies closing the RL-convergence investigation as a technical conclusion.

Stopping the expensive differentiable-QP branch is justified. Stopping the entire velocity-policy thread is premature because the reasoning chain after attempt 0 rests on a questionable—and likely incorrect—definition of the TD3 action. No experiment appears to have combined:

1. the necessary Markov observation containing velocity, and  
2. replay/critic actions defined in the same nominal-action coordinates produced by the actor.

That missing combination is more consequential than either observation normalization or reward shaping. I recommend one properly powered experiment on it before stopping.

## 1. The central problem: attempt 1 may not be a valid “fix”

The report treats storing the actor’s nominal action as a replay-buffer consistency bug. I disagree.

The actor produces nominal \(u_t\); the training loop passes that exact value to `env.step(a)` ([m6_kinodynamic_shield.py:309](/home/han/claude_project/papper-research/code/experiments/m6_kinodynamic_shield.py:309)); and the environment defines its transition by mapping that action to `v_nom` and then applying the shield ([env.py:216](/home/han/claude_project/papper-research/code/godynur/env.py:216)). Therefore the natural shielded MDP is:

\[
a_{\rm agent}=u_{\rm nominal},\qquad
s' = F_{\rm shield}(s,u_{\rm nominal}).
\]

Under that definition, the shield is part of the transition function. A transformed or saturated action is not automatically a mislabeled transition—no more than motor saturation or nonlinear actuator dynamics makes the commanded action an invalid replay label. The critic should learn \(Q(s,u_{\rm nominal})\), including the consequences of shield intervention.

Instead, attempt 1 replaces the actor’s action with a post-transition quantity,

\[
u_{\rm executed}=(v_{t+1}-v_t)/dv_{\rm scale},
\]

computed at [env.py:322](/home/han/claude_project/papper-research/code/godynur/env.py:322) and stored at [m6_kinodynamic_shield.py:311](/home/han/claude_project/papper-research/code/experiments/m6_kinodynamic_shield.py:311). But TD3’s target actor still produces nominal/raw actions at [td3.py:126](/home/han/claude_project/papper-research/code/godynur/td3.py:126). Thus the replay critic is trained in executed-action coordinates while its Bellman targets are queried in nominal-action coordinates. That is a genuine semantic mismatch.

There are additional problems with the derived executed action:

- It is clipped to `[-1,1]`, potentially aliasing physically different transitions.
- It is not the quantity the deployed actor directly selects.
- Multiple nominal requests can collapse to the same shielded action, so the critic loses information about which proposals cause intervention.
- The environment’s shield includes terminal-braking acceptance and fallback logic beyond merely achieving \(v_{t+1}\).

This reverses the report’s conclusion: storing the raw nominal action was not demonstrably a bug; storing the executed action may have introduced the more serious inconsistency.

The experiment sequence is therefore confounded:

- Attempt 0: nominal replay semantics, but velocity was missing from the observation.
- Attempt 1: executed replay semantics, still without velocity.
- Attempts 2–6: velocity is present, but the questionable executed-action semantics are retained.

No run tested “nominal replay + velocity observation,” the most basic fully observed formulation.

## 2. Attempts 5 and 6 do not repair that mismatch completely

The STE actor update evaluates the critic at the replay buffer’s historical executed action ([td3.py:145](/home/han/claude_project/papper-research/code/godynur/td3.py:145)). That is not the same as projecting the current actor output through the shield and using an identity backward derivative. It computes a gradient at a behavior action drawn from the sampled transition.

More importantly, neither STE nor the differentiable actor projection is applied to TD3’s Bellman target. Target actions remain:

```text
actor_t(s2) + smoothing noise
```

and are passed directly to the target critics at [td3.py:126](/home/han/claude_project/papper-research/code/godynur/td3.py:126). Thus the critic-target coordinate mismatch persists even when the actor-loss query is modified.

Attempt 6 is also differentiating through the box-constrained QP subproblem, not the complete deployed shield. The actual environment accepts the QP proposal only if a terminal-braking witness exists, otherwise it executes a fallback brake ([env.py:244](/home/han/claude_project/papper-research/code/godynur/env.py:244)). The differentiable projection ends after `differentiable_v_exec` ([m6_kinodynamic_shield.py:100](/home/han/claude_project/papper-research/code/experiments/m6_kinodynamic_shield.py:100)); it does not reproduce that discrete acceptance/fallback branch.

Consequently, attempt 6 demonstrates a correct differentiable implementation of one QP, but its ep200 result is very weak evidence against differentiating the full shield—and even weaker evidence against velocity-mode learning generally.

## 3. Statistical assessment

The single-seed weakness is disqualifying for claims that attempts 1–5 “do not help” across seeds. Attempt 6’s one ep200 checkpoint is not a meaningful convergence test at all.

I would require:

- **At least five independent seeds** for a screening-level negative verdict.
- Eight to ten for a paper-grade quantitative claim, consistent with the roadmap’s own standard ([KINODYNAMIC_ROADMAP.md:298](/home/han/claude_project/papper-research/code/KINODYNAMIC_ROADMAP.md:298)).

The repeated use of essentially the same seed makes the “six consecutive negative results” framing especially misleading. These are not six independent Bernoulli failures; most are correlated runs on one seed, with additive changes and a shared replay-action assumption. They prove that several configurations fail for that seed, not that each hypothesis has been population-level falsified.

Attempt 0’s five seeds do convincingly establish that the original velocity configuration fails. They do not test the corrected Markov formulation because velocity was absent.

## 4. Evaluation-protocol issue

The main evaluation routine always switches the evaluation environment to the hardest stage:

```text
env.set_difficulty(*STAGES[-1])
```

at [m6_kinodynamic_shield.py:171](/home/han/claude_project/papper-research/code/experiments/m6_kinodynamic_shield.py:171), even when the training curriculum remains at stage 0. Therefore the plotted 0–7% checkpoint success and 0.6–0.8 collision rates are hard-stage evaluations, not stage-0 evaluations.

The separate stage-0 scripted/trained diagnostic and the logged training rolling success do still establish a real base-task failure. So this does not erase the central finding. But it means:

- Hard-stage collision changes cannot diagnose stage-0 reach learning.
- The report sometimes juxtaposes “stuck at stage 0” with success/collision figures measured at stage 2.
- The proposed `dv_scale×8` explanation for increased collision is less informative about base reaching than presented.

Future diagnostic evaluation should explicitly remain at stage 0.

## 5. Observation normalization

The relevant state construction is at [env.py:425](/home/han/claude_project/papper-research/code/godynur/env.py:425). Joint state and velocity-mode features are normalized, while flange, goal and relative Cartesian vectors are in meters.

I rate this as a **plausible contributor but an unlikely primary cause**.

A concrete mechanism is possible: typical trained joint speeds are small relative to `DQ_MAX`, so `v/DQ_MAX` can often be around \(0.05\)–\(0.1\), while Cartesian errors are around \(0.2\)–\(0.4\). Early first-layer learning can therefore emphasize position and joint configuration more than velocity. A policy could learn which direction to move while learning a weak speed-dependent braking rule, producing substantial motion and repeated overshoot.

But the evidence for that mechanism is limited:

- The raw Cartesian features are already mostly within `[-1,1]`; there is no orders-of-magnitude scale problem.
- `goal-flange` directly supplies the task-relevant Cartesian error.
- The same raw Cartesian representation works for the delta-q arms.
- A 256-unit MLP can readily learn constant rescalings.
- The advisor’s actual state is not uniformly standardized either: TCP and goal are divided by 3, while the reported delta-position treatment differs ([ASSUMPTIONS.md:60](/home/han/claude_project/papper-research/code/ASSUMPTIONS.md:60)).

Also, “moves but never settles” has not actually been demonstrated as overshoot. With `goal_dwell=1` ([env.py:44](/home/han/claude_project/papper-research/code/godynur/env.py:44)), the policy succeeds upon one entry into the 5 cm sphere; it does not need to settle. Minimum distance, radial velocity at closest approach, and overshoot counts were not reported. The actor may simply wander in the wrong directions.

If normalization is tested later, empirical feature standardization is more informative than merely mapping workspace meters to `[-1,1]`.

## 6. Reward-shaping mismatch

The report is numerically correct that the exponential term is small over much of the initial state distribution, but “negligible reward signal” is too strong because `-err` remains dense.

Using the actual goal sampler, I find an initial distance distribution of approximately:

- Median: 0.282 m
- 75th percentile: 0.351 m
- 90th percentile: 0.405 m

The exponential bonus is:

| Error | \(0.5e^{-err/0.08}\) |
|---:|---:|
| 0.15 m | 0.0767 |
| 0.20 m | 0.0410 |
| 0.25 m | 0.0220 |
| 0.30 m | 0.0118 |
| 0.40 m | 0.00337 |

At the median initial distance it is about 0.015, versus a base reward of about -0.282. Thus it is indeed a small component for most early states. But the complete reward at [env.py:619](/home/han/claude_project/papper-research/code/godynur/env.py:619) still changes continuously with distance.

The advisor’s `+0.05/-0.05` step-improvement term ([ASSUMPTIONS.md:66](/home/han/claude_project/papper-research/code/ASSUMPTIONS.md:66)) could nevertheless matter substantially:

- One-step Cartesian changes under jerk-limited velocity control can be only millimetres, so differences in `-err` between candidate actions are initially tiny.
- The discrete term converts any progress/regression into a 0.1 reward separation, making immediate action discrimination much easier.
- Because it rewards the sign rather than magnitude of progress, a slowly approaching, decelerating action can receive the same shaping bonus as a fast approach. The current cumulative `-err` objective more strongly prefers getting close quickly, which can promote excessive approach velocity.
- After overshoot, increasing distance immediately flips the shaping term negative.

It is not a complete deceleration reward: braking before the closest approach may reduce progress but still gives `+0.05`, and it does not explicitly reward low radial velocity. With dwell 1, only momentary entry is required. Still, I rate the reward mismatch as a credible secondary bottleneck and more plausible than network capacity.

## 8. Additional unlisted hypothesis: time-limit handling

Timeout is included in `done` ([env.py:360](/home/han/claude_project/papper-research/code/godynur/env.py:360)) and stored directly as TD3’s terminal flag ([m6_kinodynamic_shield.py:317](/home/han/claude_project/papper-research/code/experiments/m6_kinodynamic_shield.py:317)). The observation contains no remaining-time feature.

That creates two related issues:

- The same physical state can have different value depending on whether five or eighty steps remain, but the critic cannot distinguish them.
- Treating a time-limit truncation as an absorbing terminal state suppresses bootstrapping at step 100.

This affects all arms, but it can hurt the slower double-integrator interface more. It could also encourage rushing and high-speed approaches. I would not test it before action semantics, but it should remain on the list.

## 9. One recommended experiment before stopping

Run a paired, stage-0-only A/B experiment testing the replay-action definition.

Configuration:

- Start from the attempt-2 formulation: velocity included; default `dv_scale`, noise, gamma and existing reward.
- Disable STE and differentiable-QP actor updates; use standard TD3.
- Do not add/remove reward shaping or normalization.
- Arm A stores the actor’s nominal action passed to `env.step`.
- Arm B stores the derived executed action, as attempt 2 did.
- Use five paired independent seeds, 3,000 episodes each.
- Train and evaluate at stage 0; use at least 100 fixed, common evaluation goals per checkpoint.

Measure:

- Stage-0 success.
- Minimum goal distance per episode and fractions reaching 0.20/0.10/0.05 m.
- Radial flange velocity and joint speed at closest approach.
- Overshoot count.
- Shield fallback/intervention rate.
- Learning curves per seed, not just pooled final means.

A result would support the nominal-action hypothesis if Arm A shows a clear learning trend in most seeds—for example, at least a 15 percentage-point mean success advantage, with several seeds reaching substantial success or curriculum-quality performance—while Arm B remains near zero.

It would refute the hypothesis if all five nominal-action seeds remain near the present ≤5% level through 3,000 episodes and show no improvement in minimum-distance distributions. If that happens, I would accept stopping this thread and document reward shaping, time-limit handling, normalization and scale tuning as unresolved alternatives rather than claiming they were ruled out.

**Recommendation: run this one experiment before stopping.** The current six-attempt chain does not yet test the most coherent fully observed nominal-action MDP, so accepting the commander’s conclusion now would be premature.
